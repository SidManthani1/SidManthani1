Drop Unecessary Columns¶

After looking at the data, I realize that much of it serves no purpose in answering my research questions, and that some data seems to be missing from the database. In order to make the data more relevant to my research questions I dropped the 'id', 'imdb_id', 'popularity', 'budget', 'revenue', 'original_title, 'homepage', 'tagline', 'keywords', 'overview', 'release_date' and 'release_year' columns.

The original title, homepage, tagline, keywords, and overview are different for each movie, and are therefore difficult to correlate with other factors, while the id and imbd_id do not have any relation with the rest of the data.

While the budget is not all that determines the vote_average of a movie, there may be a correlation between the budget of a movie and its score, for this reason, I use the inflation adjusted budget to see whether a higher budget means higher vote_average.

While they may affect the revenue, I felt that the release date of a movie does not factor into its vote_average.

Finally, I dropped the popularity column as popularity is partially based on the vote_average and vote_count, and is therefore not a factor in the vote_average.

Question 1: Which Genre has the Highest Mean Vote Average?¶

To answer this question, I had to first find the mean vote average of each genre. In order to make sense of my results, I created a bar chart which has bars showing the mean vote average of each genre. Even after creating the bar chart however, I could only approximately guess which genre had the largest mean vote average, and to remedy this situation I made it so the value of each bar was placed next to it.

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(15,15))

# Get the average vote_average for each genre
vote_average = df.groupby('genres')['vote_average'].mean()

y_pos = np.arange(len(df['genres'].unique()))

ax.set_yticklabels(sorted(df['genres'].unique()))

ax.set_title('Vote Average by Genre')

# Set Size of X and Y labels
plt.rc('xtick', labelsize=8)
plt.rc('ytick', labelsize=8)

# Put Values next to Each Bar
for i, v in enumerate(vote_average):
    ax.text(v, i, str(round(v,2)), color='blue', fontweight='bold')
    
ax.invert_yaxis()  # labels read top-to-bottom

# Draw Bar Chart
ax.barh(y_pos, vote_average, align='center', color='green', ecolor='black')

Characteristics of Movies with High Vote Averages¶

I first graphed runtime, vote count, budget and revenue against vote average respectively, but this did not give me much information as while the majority of the data points were in the same region, the few outliers expanded the size of the size of the graph making it hard to see trends.

To combat this problem, I set limits on the x-axis of each graph so I could get a clearer view of trends.

It turns out that most movies have runtimes of between 75 and 220 minutes, and that for movies whose time frames are in this interval, a higher runtime correlates to a higher vote average.

The second thing I found out was that the higher the vote average of a movie, the higher the vote count it is likely to have.

It also seems as if budget and vote average have a very weak positive correlation, meaning that a movie's budget does not really affect its vote average.

Finally, a movie with a higher revenue generally has a higher vote count.

Limitations¶

Some of the limitations of my analysis include:

Large portions of missing data, which brought down the total number of movies being looked at to around 4000 from nearly 11000.

Not taking into account the number of people that watched each genre. A larger number of viewers means a wider variety of opinions on a movie, and might therefore lower the vote average.

Not considering the types of people that rated these movies. Different groups of people may view the same movie differently, so the results might be skewed depending on who was rating the movies.

In addition to all of this, I couldn't look at combinations of genres, or combinations of actors that may have made a movie popular due to the fact that I couldn't think of a way to narrow down the large number of different combinations possible and see which ones meant higher vote averages.

Conclusions¶

By analyzing the data, I found that the genre with the highest mean vote average was documentary.

Next, I looked at some of the characteristics of movies with high vote averages. I found that higher vote averages correlate to higher runtimes, higher vote counts, and higher revenue. At the same time, there is only a very low positive correlation between budget and vote average meaning that a movie's budget has very little effect on its vote average.

When looking at the mean vote average of the movies starred in by each individual actor, I found that the mean of the vote averages of all the movies Chris Frantz, David Byrne, Steven Scales and Tina Weymouth starred in was the highest at 8.4.

The mean vote average of the movies produced by the Arnold Stiefel Company, and Talking Heads was the highest of all the production companies at 8.4 matching that of the actors.

Director Damien Chazelle's movies had a mean vote average of 8.2, making him the director with the highest mean vote average.
